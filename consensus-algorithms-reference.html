Consensus · Blockchain reference 09 mechanisms

Distributed systems · consensus

How networks
agree on truth.

Without a central authority, a blockchain relies on a consensus algorithm to validate transactions, order history, resist attacks, and prevent double-spends. This is a field guide to the nine mechanisms that do that job — how each one works, the phases it moves through, the states its nodes occupy, and what it trades away.

Every design answers the Blockchain Trilemma.

A network can sharpen two of security, decentralization, and scalability — but pushing all three at once, no one has cleanly solved. Each algorithm below is a different bet on which corner to give up.

The framing

The Blockchain Trilemma

A consensus algorithm is the cryptographic heart of a decentralized network — the rules by which independent nodes agree on one indisputable ledger. Every algorithm is constrained by the same tension: it can maximize at most two of the three properties below at the expense of the third. The scores beside each mechanism throughout this guide place it on exactly these three axes.

Security

Robustness against attacks, bugs, and data manipulation — how expensive it is to rewrite or censor history.

Decentralization

How widely and independently validation is distributed, and how low the barrier is to participate.

Scalability

Transaction throughput and latency — how much load the network absorbs without degrading.

01

Proof of Work PoW

Burn energy, earn trust.

The original mechanism. Nodes prove commitment by spending real computational energy to solve a cryptographic puzzle; the winner writes the next block.

CategoryPublic · Permissionless Sybil resistancePhysical hardware + electricity Fault modelHonest majority of hashpower (>50%) FinalityProbabilistic (deepens with confirmations)

How it works

Every participating miner assembles a candidate block from the mempool — a coinbase transaction, a set of pending transactions, and a header committing to their Merkle root, the previous block hash, a timestamp, the difficulty target, and a nonce.

Mining is a brute-force search: the miner repeatedly changes the nonce (and extra-nonce) and hashes the header, hunting for an output numerically below the target difficulty. Finding a valid hash is astronomically hard; verifying one is a single hash, so the rest of the network checks it near-instantly.

The first miner to find a valid hash broadcasts the block. Honest nodes validate it and extend the chain with the most accumulated work (Nakamoto consensus). Ties and short forks resolve as the heavier branch wins; losing blocks become orphans/stales.

Phases

  1. 01 Mempool collectionNodes gather and prioritise unconfirmed transactions, usually by fee density.
  2. 02 Block template assemblyTransactions are packed into a candidate block; the coinbase and Merkle root are fixed.
  3. 03 Proof search (hashing loop)Miners iterate the nonce, hashing the header until an output falls below the difficulty target.
  4. 04 PropagationThe winning block is gossiped across the peer-to-peer network.
  5. 05 Validation & extensionPeers cheaply verify the hash, transactions, and rules, then build on top of it.
  6. 06 Difficulty retargetPeriodically (e.g. every 2016 blocks in Bitcoin, ~2 weeks) the target adjusts to hold block time roughly constant.

States & transitions

Block lifecycle
candidate→mined→propagated→confirmed (N deep)→orphaned / stale

Security & attack surface

  • 51% attack. An attacker who out-hashes the honest network can reorg recent blocks to double-spend or censor. For large chains the hardware and energy cost is prohibitive.
  • Selfish mining. A miner withholding found blocks can, above a hashpower threshold, gain more than its fair share of rewards.
  • Eclipse attacks. Isolating a node's peer connections can feed it a false view of the chain.
  • ASIC / pool centralisation. Specialised hardware and large mining pools concentrate practical control despite open participation.
Trilemma position
Security 9/10
Decentralization 9/10
Scalability 2/10
At a glance
Block time~10 min
Throughput~7
EnergyVery high
FinalityProbabilistic
In the wild
  • Bitcoin (BTC)SHA-256 PoW; trades throughput for maximal security and censorship-resistance. Lightning Network is its off-chain speed layer.
  • Litecoin (LTC)Scrypt algorithm keeps mining more memory-bound and accessible; faster, lighter payments.
  • Dogecoin (DOGE)Merge-mined with Litecoin, inheriting PoW security for cheap, fast tips.
Worth knowing

Finality is never absolute — it grows with depth. The ~6-confirmation convention for Bitcoin reflects the exponentially shrinking odds of a reorg that deep.

The difficulty-adjustment feedback loop is what keeps block time stable as global hashpower rises or falls; it is arguably the quietest but most important part of the design.

02

Proof of Stake PoS

Skin in the game, not gigawatts.

Validators lock native capital as a bond. The protocol pseudo-randomly selects proposers weighted by stake, and misbehaviour is punished by slashing the bond.

CategoryPublic · Permissionless Sybil resistanceEconomic stake (locked capital) Fault modelHonest supermajority of stake (>2/3) FinalityDeterministic checkpoint finality (economic)

How it works

A validator deposits the chain's native asset into a staking contract and joins an activation queue. Once active, it becomes eligible to propose and attest to blocks.

Time is divided into slots and epochs. For each slot a proposer is chosen pseudo-randomly, weighted by stake (Ethereum uses RANDAO for randomness). A committee of other validators attests to the proposed block.

Attestations accumulate. Under a finality gadget such as Casper FFG, checkpoints that gather a two-thirds supermajority link become justified, then finalized — giving deterministic, economically-backed finality rather than the probabilistic settling of PoW.

Phases

  1. 01 Deposit & activationStake is bonded and the validator waits in the activation queue.
  2. 02 Proposer selectionA stake-weighted pseudo-random draw picks the slot's block proposer.
  3. 03 Block proposalThe proposer builds and signs the block for its slot.
  4. 04 AttestationA committee votes on the head of the chain and the proposed block.
  5. 05 Justification & finalizationTwo-thirds attestation on checkpoints justifies then finalizes them.
  6. 06 Rewards, penalties & exitHonest work is rewarded; equivocation or downtime is slashed; validators can queue to exit.

States & transitions

Validator lifecycle (Ethereum-style)
deposited→pending / queued→active→exiting / slashed→exited→withdrawable

Security & attack surface

  • Nothing-at-stake. Historically validators could cheaply vote on every fork. Solved by slashing, which makes equivocation destroy the bond.
  • Long-range attack. Old keys could rewrite deep history; mitigated by weak-subjectivity checkpoints that new nodes trust on sync.
  • Stake grinding. Attempts to bias the randomness source in one's favour; countered by RANDAO/VDF hardening.
  • Stake concentration. Wealth and liquid-staking providers can centralise influence — the 'rich get richer' critique.
Trilemma position
Security 8/10
Decentralization 6/10
Scalability 7/10
At a glance
Block time~12 sec
Throughput~15–100
EnergyVery low
Finality~2 epochs (~13 min)
In the wild
  • Ethereum (ETH)A world computer needing sustainable security and a huge validator set. L2s (Arbitrum, Optimism, Base) supply throughput.
  • Cardano (ADA)Peer-reviewed Ouroboros targets provable security with formal-methods-friendly contracts.
  • Polkadot (DOT)Nominated PoS secures a relay chain that shares security across many specialised parachains.
Worth knowing

Slashing is the linchpin: it converts an abstract economic assumption into an enforceable penalty, turning capital-at-risk into real Sybil resistance.

Weak subjectivity is the honest trade PoS makes for finality — a freshly syncing node must trust a recent checkpoint rather than deriving everything from genesis as in PoW.

03

Delegated Proof of Stake DPoS

A blockchain democracy for speed.

Token holders elect a small set of block producers who take turns forging blocks. Absolute decentralisation is traded for raw, coordinated throughput.

CategoryPublic · Permissionless (representative) Sybil resistanceToken-weighted votes Fault modelHonest 2/3+1 of the elected producer set FinalityFast (BFT-style producer confirmation)

How it works

Token holders continuously vote for delegates (block producers / witnesses), with voting power proportional to their token balance. Votes can be redirected at any time.

The top-N candidates by votes form the active producer set for each round. Producers are shuffled into a schedule and take turns creating blocks in round-robin order.

Because only a small, known set must coordinate, blocks confirm in sub-second time. Many DPoS chains layer a BFT step so that once 2/3 of producers confirm a block it becomes irreversible (the 'last irreversible block').

Phases

  1. 01 Continuous votingHolders stake votes toward delegates; standings update in real time.
  2. 02 Delegate electionThe top-N vote-getters become the active producers for the round.
  3. 03 Schedule shuffleProducers are randomised into a turn order to prevent predictable targeting.
  4. 04 Round-robin productionEach producer forges its scheduled block in sequence.
  5. 05 BFT confirmationA 2/3 producer supermajority marks the block irreversible.
  6. 06 Rotation / recallUnderperforming or malicious delegates are voted out and replaced.

States & transitions

Delegate lifecycle
candidate→elected (active)→standby→scheduled → produced / missed→recalled

Security & attack surface

  • Cartel collusion. A colluding majority of the small producer set can censor or reorder — the central risk of DPoS.
  • Voter apathy. Low participation lets a minority of tokens entrench the same producers.
  • Vote buying. Producers can bribe holders with a share of rewards, distorting the election.
  • Attribution upside. Any misbehaviour is publicly attributable and reversible by the vote, unlike a silent PoW/PoS attack.
Trilemma position
Security 7/10
Decentralization 4/10
Scalability 9/10
At a glance
Block time~0.5–3 sec
Throughput~1,000–4,000
EnergyVery low
FinalitySub-second (BFT-DPoS)
In the wild
  • EOS21 elected block producers deliver sub-second blocks for high-throughput dApps.
  • TRON (TRX)27 super representatives keep transfers cheap and fast for high-volume content and stablecoins.
Worth knowing

The producer count is the core tuning knob: fewer producers means faster coordination but weaker decentralisation, and vice-versa.

Standby producers and continuous re-election give DPoS a social recovery path that purely cryptographic mechanisms lack.

04

Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance PBFT

Vote to agreement, tolerate traitors.

A known, permissioned set of nodes exchanges structured votes across multiple rounds to agree on order, tolerating up to one-third Byzantine participants with instant finality.

CategoryEnterprise · Permissioned Sybil resistanceIdentity / membership control Fault modeln ≥ 3f + 1 — tolerates f Byzantine nodes FinalityDeterministic & immediate on commit

How it works

PBFT solves the Byzantine Generals Problem: reaching agreement when some participants may lie. Nodes are identified and permissioned, and one acts as the primary (leader) for the current view.

A client request flows through a three-phase agreement — pre-prepare, prepare, commit — where replicas exchange signed messages and count matching votes to reach quorums. Once a replica collects a commit quorum it executes the request and replies.

If the primary is slow or faulty, replicas trigger a view change to elect a new primary. Periodic checkpoints let nodes garbage-collect old messages and agree on a stable state.

Phases

  1. 01 RequestA client sends an operation to the primary.
  2. 02 Pre-prepareThe primary assigns a sequence number and broadcasts the request to all replicas.
  3. 03 PrepareReplicas broadcast prepare messages; collecting 2f matching marks the request 'prepared'.
  4. 04 CommitReplicas broadcast commit messages; a 2f+1 quorum makes it 'committed-local'.
  5. 05 ReplyEach replica executes and replies; the client accepts on f+1 matching replies.
  6. 06 View change / checkpointA faulty primary triggers re-election; stable checkpoints prune history.

States & transitions

Request state machine
pre-prepared→prepared→committed-local→executed→(view-change)

Security & attack surface

  • One-third bound. Safety and liveness hold as long as fewer than n/3 nodes are Byzantine; beyond that, no guarantees.
  • O(n²) message overhead. All-to-all voting means communication grows quadratically, capping practical network size.
  • Primary targeting. The leader is a DoS focal point, mitigated by timeouts and view changes.
  • Closed membership. Security depends entirely on the authority that admits nodes — there is no open Sybil resistance.
Trilemma position
Security 9/10
Decentralization 3/10
Scalability 8/10
At a glance
Block timeInstant finality
Throughput~1,000–10,000
EnergyVery low
FinalityImmediate on commit
In the wild
  • Hyperledger FabricConsortia need known participants, instant finality, and privacy — a natural fit for BFT ordering.
  • Zilliqa (ZIL)Combines PoW for initial Sybil resistance with PBFT inside sharded committees for high TPS.
Worth knowing

PBFT is the ancestor of modern BFT consensus. Tendermint, HotStuff, IBFT, and SBFT all descend from it, mostly attacking its quadratic message cost.

Because finality is immediate and deterministic, there are no forks or reorgs to reason about — a major operational simplification for enterprises.

05

Proof of Authority PoA

Reputation is the stake.

A pre-approved set of identified validators signs blocks in turn. What is staked is real-world identity and reputation, not capital or compute.

CategoryEnterprise · Permissioned Sybil resistanceVetted legal identity Fault modelHonest majority of authorised signers FinalityFast · effectively immediate

How it works

Validators are vetted and explicitly authorised through governance; each node is tied to a known real-world identity, so any fraud is directly attributable.

Authorised signers seal blocks on a fixed schedule. In the Clique scheme, an 'in-turn' signer produces with priority weight, while 'out-of-turn' signers may step in after a small randomised delay if the scheduled signer is absent.

Governance votes add or remove authorities. A misbehaving signer is deauthorised and loses both its privileges and its reputation — the entire deterrent of the scheme.

Phases

  1. 01 AuthorisationGovernance admits a vetted identity into the signer set.
  2. 02 Sealing scheduleSigners are ordered; in-turn vs out-of-turn priority is assigned per slot.
  3. 03 Block sealingThe scheduled authority signs and produces the block.
  4. 04 Propagation & acceptancePeers accept blocks signed by a currently-authorised key.
  5. 05 Governance voteSigners vote to add or evict authorities as needed.

States & transitions

Authority lifecycle
proposed→authorised (in-turn / out-of-turn)→sealing→deauthorised

Security & attack surface

  • Authority compromise. A stolen or coerced signer key is the primary threat; mitigated by fast eviction.
  • Collusion & censorship. A majority of signers can censor, with only reputational — not economic — cost.
  • Central trust. Unsuitable for permissionless settings: you must trust the admitting authority.
  • Full Sybil resistance. Identity vetting makes fake nodes impossible, which is precisely why decentralisation is minimal.
Trilemma position
Security 8/10
Decentralization 1/10
Scalability 9/10
At a glance
Block time~1–5 sec
Throughput~1,000+
EnergyNegligible
FinalityNear-immediate
In the wild
  • VeChain (VET)Supply-chain enterprises want fast, predictable, low-cost blocks from known authority nodes.
  • Ethereum PoA testnetsClique-based networks (historically Görli, Rinkeby, Kovan) gave developers a cheap, stable environment without staking economics.
Worth knowing

PoA can be seen as PoS with identity substituted for capital: the bond is a reputation that cannot be re-bought once burned.

The two dominant implementations are Clique (Ethereum) and Aura (Parity), differing mainly in how they schedule signers and handle equivocation.

06

Proof of History PoH

A cryptographic clock for consensus.

Not a standalone consensus, but a verifiable clock layered on PoS. A continuous sequential hash proves the order and spacing of events so validators need not message each other to agree on time.

CategoryPublic · Permissionless (hybrid) Sybil resistanceInherited from paired PoS layer Fault modelHonest supermajority of PoS stake FinalityFast optimistic confirmation → rooted

How it works

A leader runs a sequential, non-parallelisable hash function (a verifiable delay function) where each output feeds the next, producing an unforgeable timeline. The count of hashes between two events proves how much time elapsed.

A PoS-derived leader schedule rotates which validator produces during each slot. The active leader stamps incoming transactions into the hash sequence at specific counts, so their order is cryptographically fixed before any voting happens.

Validators vote on the resulting chain using a BFT layer (Solana's Tower BFT applies lockouts to votes). Removing the need to negotiate time is what unlocks the extreme throughput; security still comes from the underlying stake.

Phases

  1. 01 Continuous hash sequenceThe leader generates an unbroken VDF timeline.
  2. 02 Leader schedulePoS determines which validator leads each slot.
  3. 03 Transaction stampingIncoming transactions are recorded at specific hash counts, fixing order.
  4. 04 Entry / block productionStamped entries are batched into blocks.
  5. 05 Voting (Tower BFT)Validators vote with exponential lockouts to confirm the chain.
  6. 06 Finalization (rooting)Sufficiently confirmed blocks become rooted and irreversible.

States & transitions

Slot & block states
leader / validator (per slot)→processed→confirmed (optimistic)→rooted

Security & attack surface

  • Inherited stake attacks. PoH only orders time; the money-at-risk model is standard PoS underneath.
  • Hardware centralisation. Keeping up with the hash timeline demands fast, high-end servers, raising the participation barrier.
  • Liveness under load. Extreme throughput has historically stressed the network into outages during congestion.
  • Leader dependence. A faulty or offline leader forces the schedule to skip and recover.
Trilemma position
Security 7/10
Decentralization 5/10
Scalability 10/10
At a glance
Block time~0.4 sec
Throughput~2,000–65,000
EnergyLow
FinalityFast optimistic
In the wild
  • Solana (SOL)Consumer-scale apps demanding tens of thousands of TPS; PoH removes the timing bottleneck that limits other chains.
Worth knowing

PoH is best understood as pre-consensus ordering: it does not decide truth, it decides sequence, which is the expensive part everyone else negotiates at runtime.

It pairs with a stack of supporting innovations (leader schedules, Turbine block propagation, Gulf Stream mempool-forwarding) — PoH alone is not the whole story.

07

Proof of Elapsed Time PoET

A fair lottery, sealed in silicon.

Each node draws a random wait time from trusted hardware; the shortest waiter wins the block. A lottery that delivers PoW-like fairness without the energy burn.

CategoryEnterprise · Permissioned Sybil resistanceAttested hardware identity Fault modelHonest majority + trusted TEE FinalityConfigurable (typically fast)

How it works

Developed by Intel, PoET has each node request a random wait time from a Trusted Execution Environment (TEE) such as Intel SGX. The enclave guarantees the timer was genuinely random and actually observed.

Every node sleeps for its assigned duration. Whichever node's timer expires first wakes and claims the right to build the next block, attaching a hardware attestation as proof.

Other nodes verify the attestation before accepting the block. Implementations such as Sawtooth add statistical fairness checks (a z-test) to detect nodes that win improbably often, catching a compromised enclave.

Phases

  1. 01 Enclave registrationA node joins by proving a valid, attested SGX enclave.
  2. 02 Timer requestThe enclave issues a signed random wait time.
  3. 03 WaitThe node sleeps for its assigned duration.
  4. 04 ClaimThe shortest waiter wakes and proposes the block with attestation.
  5. 05 Verify & fairness checkPeers validate the attestation and run statistical anti-cheat tests.

States & transitions

Node states
registered (attested)→waiting (timer)→won → proposing→expired

Security & attack surface

  • TEE compromise. The whole model rests on trusted hardware; SGX vulnerabilities (Foreshadow, Plundervolt, etc.) are the core risk.
  • Vendor trust. You must trust a single manufacturer's silicon and attestation service.
  • Enclave Sybil. Fairness checks are needed because a broken enclave could forge many favourable timers.
  • Permissioned scope. Realistically confined to enterprise deployments where hardware can be controlled.
Trilemma position
Security 8/10
Decentralization 4/10
Scalability 8/10
At a glance
Block timeConfigurable
Throughput~1,000s
EnergyVery low
FinalityConfigurable
In the wild
  • Hyperledger SawtoothEnterprises get PoW-like fairness at near-zero energy by leaning on secure hardware timers.
Worth knowing

PoET's fairness statistically approximates PoW's lottery — same probabilistic winner selection, without the electricity.

Sawtooth ships both a real PoET-SGX engine and a PoET-simulator for development environments without SGX hardware.

08

Proof of Space / Capacity PoC

Mine with storage, not with heat.

Miners pre-compute large datasets and store them on disk. Whoever holds the closest matching 'plot' for a challenge wins. Physical space replaces physical energy.

CategoryPublic · Permissionless Sybil resistanceCommitted disk capacity Fault modelHonest majority of storage FinalityProbabilistic (space + optional time proof)

How it works

In a one-time plotting step, miners fill hard drives with large tables of cryptographic hashes (proofs of space). This is compute-heavy once, then cheap to reuse.

For each block the network issues a challenge. Farmers scan their stored plots for the value that best answers it; more allocated storage means a higher probability of holding a winning answer.

The best proof earns the right to create the block, and verification by the rest of the network is cheap. Chia pairs proofs of space with a proof of time (a VDF) so that blocks are also spaced correctly in wall-clock time.

Phases

  1. 01 PlottingA one-time pass writes proof tables to disk.
  2. 02 ChallengeThe network issues a per-block challenge value.
  3. 03 Scanning / lookupFarmers search their plots for a qualifying proof.
  4. 04 Proof submissionThe best-quality proof is broadcast.
  5. 05 Verify & createPeers cheaply verify; the winner builds the block (with a VDF time proof in Chia).

States & transitions

Plot & farmer states
plotting→plotted / available→idle → scanning→winning proof

Security & attack surface

  • 51% storage. An attacker must control more raw storage than the honest network — cheaper than PoW hashpower, still capital-heavy.
  • Plotting arms race. Competition for space drives ever-larger farms, echoing PoW's hardware race.
  • Storage wear. Constant plotting and re-plotting rapidly degrades SSDs, shifting cost from energy to e-waste.
  • Grinding on challenges. Naïve designs allow re-plotting to bias outcomes; countered by pairing with a VDF.

In the wild
  • Chia (XCH)A greener, commodity-hardware alternative to Bitcoin using farmed storage plus proofs of time.

Worth knowing

The family spans subtly different ideas: Proof of Capacity, Proof of Space, and Chia's Proof of Space and Time, which adds the VDF to fix timing.

Its selling point is repurposing hardware people already own, but the SSD-attrition problem complicates the 'green' narrative.

09

Proof of Burn PoB

Destroy coins to earn the right to mine.

Miners permanently send coins to an unspendable address. That verifiable, irreversible sacrifice grants proportional mining rights — a virtual, energy-free investment in the network.

CategoryPublic · Permissionless Sybil resistanceVerifiably destroyed capital Fault modelHonest majority of burned value FinalityDepends on layered base mechanism

How it works

A miner sends coins to a provably unspendable 'eater' address — a public, on-chain, irreversible commitment that anyone can verify but no one can recover.

The protocol grants mining rights in proportion to the amount burned. Many designs decay burned weight over time, so miners must keep burning to stay competitive, mimicking the ongoing cost of PoW hardware.

Holders of mining rights then produce blocks and earn ongoing rewards. Committed capital is destroyed rather than spent on electricity or silicon, making the mechanism energy-free by construction.

Phases

  1. 01 Burn transactionCoins are sent to a provably unspendable address.
  2. 02 On-chain recordThe burn is logged as a public, irreversible commitment.
  3. 03 Rights grantMining power is assigned proportional to the burn (often with decay).
  4. 04 Block productionRights-holders forge blocks and collect rewards.

States & transitions

Commitment states
coins burned→recorded on-chain→rights active (decaying)→expired

Security & attack surface

  • Dominance cost. Controlling the chain means permanently burning more value than everyone combined — a fully sunk expense.
  • Economic manipulation. Artificial scarcity from burning can distort token economics.
  • External dependency. Some designs require burning an established coin (e.g. Bitcoin) to bootstrap a new chain.
  • Sybil resistance. Influence is tied to destroyed capital, which cannot be cheaply forged.

Security 7/10
Decentralization 6/10
Scalability 4/10

In the wild
  • Slimcoin (SLM)Uses burning as a long-term, energy-free commitment layered with PoW and PoS ideas.
Worth knowing

PoB is arguably the purest 'skin in the game': the stake can never be recovered, even for a perfectly honest participant.

It is distinct from modern fee-burning (e.g. Ethereum's EIP-1559), which destroys coins for monetary policy rather than to grant consensus rights.

A closing distinction

Public vs. enterprise blockchains

The sharpest way to organise all nine mechanisms is by whether participants are anonymous or known. That single fact decides which trade-offs a chain can afford.

Open · anonymous

Public, permissionless

Anyone can join, so the network must be trustless. Sybil resistance has to be bought with a scarce real resource — energy, capital, storage, or destroyed value — and finality is usually probabilistic or economically enforced.

PoWPoSDPoSPoHPoCPoB
Closed · identified

Enterprise, permissioned

Participants are legally known, so trustlessness is unnecessary. That lets these chains use highly efficient voting or hardware mechanisms with instant finality — at the cost of open decentralization.

PBFTPoAPoET

Every mechanism here answers one question in a different currency: who do we trust, and what do we make them pay for that trust? Energy, capital, reputation, hardware, or destroyed coins. The choice of algorithm is really a statement about which currency of trust a network can afford.
